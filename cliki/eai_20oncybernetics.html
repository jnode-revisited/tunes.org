General Cybernetics is the science of information exchanging systems. As for the "distribution of the responsibilities", I think that you make an error, which is to believe that the responsibilities are added and subdivide themselves. Your argument could be also applied for the WWII: the French Generals could better have prepared France; the politicians could have prevented the catastrophe; wouldn't the Nazis thus be responsible for all their victims? Eh what if! and that without forgiving the French persons in charge at all: the fact that the sum of the responsibilities exceeds (and largely) the sum of the events is the index of the interaction between the people and their decisions; the sums correspond only for people isolated from/to each other. The fact that so-and-so is responsible for such event (i.e. that being given HIS information and ITS means, it had average to make a decision influencing largely the aforementioned event), does not prevent that other people are also somewhat responsible for the same event, since the responsibility will move with the point of view of each person.
